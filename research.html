Research

My research asks how interactive systems can support the informal, human side of communication and everyday life — from casual chat in remote work to how we perceive and organize our physical environments.

Designing and Evaluating Interfaces that Encourage Participation in Casual Chat in Remote-Work Environments

Master's Thesis · 2020–2022 IIS Lab, The University of Tokyo Advisor: Prof. Koji Yatani

COVID-19 made remote work the norm — and with it came isolation and a weakened sense of belonging, as workplaces lost the casual chat that informally connects people. This research approached that social problem through HCI: what information should an interface present to encourage people to join a casual conversation?

Building on prior CSCW work, I constructed an operational definition of casual chat, then designed and conducted a 12-participant semi-structured interview study spanning students, employees, executives, and freelancers (ages 20s–60s). Qualitative analysis identified which on-screen cues motivate participation — names/icons plus recent-status keywords encouraged joining, while full conversation transcripts backfired — and I distilled these findings into design requirements for chat interfaces that invite, rather than pressure, participation.

Sano, S., Sato Jiengjiela, A., & Yatani, K. (2021). A Qualitative Study of Interface Design to Support Casual Chat in Remote-Work Environments. IPSJ SIG Technical Report (HCI), vol. 2021-HCI-195, no. 29, pp. 1–8.

A New Dataset and Benchmark for Evaluating Messy Rooms

Bachelor's Thesis · 2019–2020 Dept. of Aeronautics & Astronautics, The University of Tokyo Advisors: Prof. Koichi Hori · Prof. Takehisa Yairi

What makes a room look "messy" — and can a machine learn to see it the way we do? I built a dataset of tidy and untidy room images grounded in public consensus, proposed a machine-learning method with custom features to learn tidiness, and ran gaze- and behavior-tracking user studies to analyze how tidiness perception relates to organizing skills. I also explored generating "tidied-up" versions of messy-room images.
